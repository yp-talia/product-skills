**Context:** Series A consumer legaltech — cohabitation agreements, financial settlement guidance, and behavioural science–driven dispute resolution. The user flagged the cohabitation agreement builder as their shakiest bet.

---

## 🔍 Market context

Amicable, now backed by Octopus Group after a majority stake acquisition in December 2023, is expanding its service range — including co-parenting tools and integrated financial services. Meanwhile, Wenup is already live with a collaborative prenup platform at £1,380 fixed fee and has launched cohabitation agreements at £499.50 per person. They're not in the roadmap's competitive analysis, but they're the closest thing to a direct competitor in the upstream relationship-planning space. DivorceIQ has also entered the market with a financial settlement forecasting tool (priced from £49), offering scenario modelling and cashflow projections — which overlaps directly with the "What's fair?" tool. On the regulatory front, the cohabitation consultation is confirmed for Spring 2026 — potentially months earlier than the roadmap assumes.

## ✓ What's actually working

1. The behavioural science positioning is genuinely differentiated. No competitor is embedding de-biasing techniques, anchoring correction, or validated emotional measurement into the product. The STAI-6 integration (a validated anxiety measurement instrument) is smart because it creates a defensible dataset, not just a feature.
2. The roadmap traces cleanly to the 6-month success metrics. The adaptive decision-support engine links directly to NRR 115%. The cohabitation builder links to the £400K ARR target. The regulatory tracker links to the 8-week readiness goal.
3. The Now/Next/Later structure is appropriate for Series A, and the sequencing within NOW is sound — each item makes the next one more valuable.

## 🔥 The roast

*Most roadmaps at this stage have some version of these patterns — this isn't a verdict, it's a navigation guide.*

🔴 **The biggest growth bet has no discovery runway.** The cohabitation agreement builder is scheduled for Q3 and expected to drive £400K ARR. But there's nothing in Q2 that validates whether cohabiting couples will actually pay £150–£500 for a digitally guided agreement, what triggers them to act, or whether the behavioural science approach resonates with this demographically different audience. You're committing a full quarter of engineering to a product whose ICP assumptions are untested.

🟡 **The competitive landscape has a blind spot.** Wenup is live, collaborative, digitally native, and already offering cohabitation agreements at £499.50/person. DivorceIQ is offering financial settlement scenario modelling at £97. These aren't existential threats, but if the board Googles the space and finds them, the roadmap should already have an answer.

🟡 **The NRR target is ambitious but the mechanism is under-specified.** Going from 98% to 115% NRR in six months means users who came for divorce filing need to start paying meaningfully more for financial settlement guidance. That's a pricing and packaging problem as much as a product problem — but the roadmap only addresses the product side.

🟢 **The regulatory consultation may land earlier than you think.** The roadmap assumes H2 2026, but the latest parliamentary signal is Spring 2026. Consider pulling the regulatory tracker into late Q2 as a monitoring capability.

## 💡 The question worth sitting with

If the cohabitation agreement product reaches £400K ARR by September, which three things will have been true about those customers — how they found you, what triggered them to act, and what made them choose a behavioural science platform over a £30 template — and can you articulate those three things today with any evidence?

## 🛠 Three craft improvements

1. For the cohabitation builder specifically, write down the three assumptions that would kill the bet if wrong. Start each one with "This only works if..."
2. Add a "what changes for the user" sentence to each NEXT and LATER item. Right now, those horizons read more like strategic positioning statements than product outcomes.
3. Separate your competitive response from your roadmap. For each competitor, write one sentence: "We win against [X] because [roadmap item Y] creates [specific advantage Z]."
