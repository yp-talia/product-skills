# Decisions

Design choices behind these skills and why they're built the way they are.

---

## Self-assessment before research (Roadmap Roast)

The skill asks "what feels shakiest to you about this roadmap right now?" before doing any research or evaluation.

This isn't a warm-up question. It reduces defensiveness — when the roast confirms something the user already suspected, it lands as validation rather than attack. It also reveals political context (what they name often signals what's sensitive) and sharpens fluency calibration (their answer reveals how they think about product). Skipping this step produces a technically correct critique that people resist acting on.

---

## Silent fluency calibration (Roadmap Roast)

The skill reads the user's input for product fluency signals and adjusts its vocabulary accordingly — but never tells the user it's doing this.

Announcing "I've assessed you as low fluency" would be patronising and counterproductive. The output should simply feel right: a founder with no product background gets plain language and consequences ("you'll build this and won't know if it worked"), while a Series B VP of Product gets full vocabulary ("the discovery evidence is thin on your far-horizon bets"). Same insight, different register.

---

## Three severity levels, not five (Roadmap Roast)

Roast points are triaged 🔴/🟡/🟢 — three levels, not the five you'd typically see in a risk matrix.

Five levels create false precision. When you tell someone their roadmap has a "4 out of 5 severity" issue, they spend energy debating whether it's really a 3. Three levels force a clear action signal: fix before your next board meeting (🔴), fix in your next planning cycle (🟡), or watch but don't panic (🟢). The user finishes reading knowing exactly what to do first.

---

## Inline citations, not grouped (all research skills)

Every factual claim gets its source placed immediately next to it — never grouped at the end of a paragraph or section.

Grouped citations look tidy but are functionally useless. If a paragraph contains four claims and lists three sources at the bottom, the reader can't tell which source supports which claim. This matters because research output gets used in high-stakes conversations (interviews, board meetings, investment decisions) where individual claims get challenged. The reader needs to verify any single statement without hunting.

---

## Negative claims require extra rigour (all research skills)

Stating that something does *not* exist (a company isn't hiring, a competitor doesn't offer a feature, no regulation is pending) requires checking at least 3 independent sources.

Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. Most research errors that cause embarrassment are negative claims: "they're not hiring" when you're interviewing there, "no competitors serve this segment" when the interviewer's previous company did exactly that. Requiring 3-source verification for negative claims catches these before they become problems. When 3 sources can't be checked, the phrasing shifts from "does not exist" to "could not confirm" — a small change that avoids overconfident claims.

---

## Chrome recommended, not required (all research skills)

Chrome browser automation is listed as "recommended" rather than "required", with explicit guidance to note what's missing from the output when Chrome isn't available.

Making Chrome required would mean the skills fail entirely without it. Making Chrome silently optional would mean the skills produce incomplete output without telling the reader what's missing. The middle path — recommended, with graceful degradation and transparency — means the skills work everywhere but the user can make an informed choice about depth. A Glassdoor-free company intelligence report is still useful; it's just not complete.

---

## The Interview Test as a verification heuristic (all research skills)

Before writing any claim, the skills ask: "If someone stated this in a meeting with an insider, could they be easily contradicted?"

This is a more useful verification test than "is this true?" because it accounts for context and confidence. A claim might be technically sourced but still embarrassing if the source is outdated, the methodology is weak, or the insider has contradicting information. The Interview Test catches overconfident claims, stale data, and inferences presented as facts — which are the errors that actually damage credibility.
