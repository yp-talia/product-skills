# Source Referencing & Triangulation Rules

These rules apply to all research skills in this collection. Every skill should read this document before beginning research.

---

## Source Referencing

### Citation Format

Every factual claim must include an inline citation.

Format: `[Claim text] [Source: Name, "Title if applicable", URL, accessed YYYY-MM-DD]`

Example:
> The company raised £12m in Series A funding in March 2025 [Source: TechCrunch, "Amicable raises £12m", techcrunch.com/2025/03/15/..., accessed 2026-03-25]

### Source Quality Tiers

| Tier | Type | Examples | Use For |
|------|------|----------|---------|
| 1 (Primary) | Official company communications | Company website, press releases, Companies House filings, investor decks, company social media | Facts about the company's own operations |
| 2 (Authoritative) | Established press, analysts, regulatory bodies | TechCrunch, Financial Times, Gartner, FCA, government publications | Market data, industry trends, regulation |
| 3 (Informed) | Employee/customer reviews, professional social | Glassdoor, G2, Trustpilot, Which?, Google Reviews, reviews.io, LinkedIn posts | Sentiment, culture, product quality signals |
| 4 (Contextual) | Forums, blogs, social media, unverified | Reddit, Twitter/X, personal blogs, Quora | Directional signals only — flag as lower-confidence |

### Rules

1. **Always cite the source** — no unsourced factual claims
2. **Note the tier** when the source is Tier 3 or 4 — helps the reader calibrate confidence
3. **Note both dates** — publication date and access date. Flag information older than 12 months as potentially stale
4. **Prefer higher-tier sources** when multiple sources cover the same fact
5. **Flag single-source claims** — if only one source exists for an important claim, note it as a triangulation gap

## Triangulation Rules

Triangulation means verifying a claim through multiple independent sources. Not every claim needs it — but high-stakes claims do.

### Company Self-Reported Information

**If the company says something about itself on its own website or official social media, treat it as true.** This includes: what the product does, company mission/values, team descriptions, founding story, business model, stated partnerships, and pricing. These are Tier 1 sources and do not require independent verification.

Triangulation is only required when:
- External sources (Tier 2–4) make claims about the company
- You are assessing things the company might present selectively (e.g. financial health, growth trajectory, culture as experienced by employees vs as stated on the careers page)

### What Must Be Triangulated (minimum 2 independent sources)

| Claim Type | Why | Example |
|------------|-----|---------|
| Financial health (external sources) | Affects growth potential assessment | "The company is profitable" — verify via filings + press |
| Culture as experienced (vs stated) | Affects alignment assessment | "Employees report high autonomy" — verify via Glassdoor + LinkedIn posts |
| Market size and growth | Affects strategic positioning | "TAM of £2bn" — verify via 2 independent estimates/methodologies |
| Competitive positioning | Affects narrative strategy | "Market leader in X" — verify via market share data + analyst reports |
| Team/org structure (inferred) | Affects role assessment | "Product team of 15" — verify via LinkedIn headcount + job postings |
| Revenue/traction (external sources) | Affects growth potential | "£5m ARR" — verify via press + Companies House + investor mentions |

### What Does Not Need Triangulation

- Anything the company states about itself on its own website or official social media (Tier 1)
- Feature descriptions from the product itself
- Pricing from the company's own pricing page (note date accessed)
- Individual's job title from their LinkedIn profile
- Company founding date, location, basic facts

### Handling Triangulation Failures

**Single source for a high-stakes claim:**
1. State the claim with the single source
2. Flag it: "⚠️ Single source — could not independently verify"
3. Note it as something to validate through other means

**Sources contradict each other:**
1. State both versions with their sources
2. Assess which is more likely reliable (based on tier, recency, specificity)
3. Flag it: "⚠️ Conflicting information — [Source A] says X, [Source B] says Y"

## Verification Rules

These rules catch the most common and damaging research errors. They apply on top of the triangulation rules above.

### Negative Claims Require Extra Rigour

A negative claim is any assertion that something does *not* exist, is *not* happening, or *cannot* be found. Examples: "the company is not currently hiring", "no regulatory changes are pending", "there are no customer complaints about X".

Negative claims are especially dangerous because the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. If you checked one source and didn't find something, that means you didn't find it there — it does not mean it doesn't exist.

**Rules for negative claims:**
1. Never state a negative claim unless you have checked at least 3 independent sources and found nothing across all of them
2. Even then, phrase it as an observation rather than a fact: "No open roles were found on the careers page, LinkedIn Jobs, or Indeed as of [date]" — not "The company is not hiring"
3. If a negative claim would be significant to the reader, flag it with ⚠️ and note exactly which sources you checked
4. Consider whether your search methodology could have missed something. Many companies use external ATS platforms that host job listings on a subdomain or separate URL not linked from the main careers page

### Quantitative Claims Need Methodology

When stating numbers (market size, revenue, headcount, growth rates), always show how you arrived at the figure. A number without methodology looks precise but may be fabricated or misleading.

- If citing a source's number, cite the source and its methodology if available
- If estimating, state your assumptions explicitly and flag the estimate as such
- If two methodologies produce different numbers, report both and note the discrepancy
- A well-reasoned "insufficient data to estimate reliably" is always better than a precise-looking number built on weak foundations

### The Interview Test

Before writing any claim into the final output, mentally simulate someone stating it in a meeting with someone who works at the company. If the insider could easily contradict the claim, either verify it more thoroughly, soften the language, or flag it as uncertain.

This test catches the most embarrassing errors: getting the team structure wrong, misidentifying the tech stack, or citing outdated financial data as current.

## Conciseness Over Completeness

Research output should be opinionated, well-sourced, and concise. The reader needs signal, not volume.

**Target length**: 1,500–2,500 words per research output.

**Prioritise**:
- Insights that would change how you think about the company, product, or market
- Facts that are surprising, non-obvious, or hard to find
- Contradictions and open questions worth probing

**Deprioritise**:
- Information easily found on page 1 of the company website
- Generic industry context the reader likely already knows
- Exhaustive lists where a curated selection with commentary would be more useful
- Repeating the same point from multiple angles

When in doubt, ask: "Would cutting this paragraph make the output less useful?" If the answer is no, cut it.
